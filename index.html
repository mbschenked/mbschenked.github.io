Approach

Design Philosophy

Every system I build is designed to answer a question: what does the player get to feel here? Mechanics exist in service of experience — and experience has to be earned through iteration.

⚓

Finish What You Start

Pre-production is easy. The production cycle is where most projects die. I treat shipping as a design constraint — every decision gets weighed against "does this get us to a releasable state?"

📊

Systems Over Spectacle

I approach design like an engineering problem — isolate variables, define rules clearly, let emergent complexity arise from simple, well-tested foundations. Data tables, not hard-coded magic numbers.

🔄

Show the Process

A portfolio shows results. But what convinces a team to hire you is knowing how you think. I document design decisions, iteration, and rationale — not just the final screenshots.

What This Demonstrates

Taking a project from concept to a polished, shareable demo — solo, without a team to catch gaps. The ability to design, implement, and iterate on interconnected systems (combat, animation, stats) simultaneously. A command of data-driven architecture that would scale to a full production pipeline.

Design Decisions

The thinking behind the systems — not just what was built, but why.

Decision 01

Data Tables, Not Hard-Coded Values

All attack properties — damage, stagger, hitstop duration — live in spreadsheet-backed data tables. This let me tune 40+ attack variants without touching code, compressing iteration loops from hours to minutes.

Decision 02

Frame-Precise Deflection Window

The deflection system uses a narrow timing window deliberately. Tight windows force player skill investment — and the visual + haptic payoff when it lands makes success feel earned rather than accidental.

Decision 03

Weighted Action Probability on Boss AI

Instead of scripted attack sequences, boss AI uses weighted random selection from a pool of contextual actions. This makes each encounter feel distinct while keeping the fight legible and design-steerable.

Core Systems

Each system is modular and independently iterable.

⚡

Combo System

Chained light and heavy attack sequences with movement-adaptive inputs and weapon-specific behaviour trees. Designed for expressive chaining without sacrificing readability.

🛡

Deflection System

Frame-precise deflection windows trigger QTE-driven counter sequences and finisher states. Rewards skilled play with a spectacular visual payoff.

🔄

Stat & State System

Data-driven player attribute tables govern hit reactions, stagger thresholds, and stamina gating — all exposed to designers via spreadsheet for rapid tuning without recompiling.

🏃

Locomotion System

8-directional state machine driven by speed, movement angle, and weapon stance. Covers idle turn-in-place, jog start/stop, guard strafe, aim locomotion — blended with orientation warping and procedural foot IK.

Design Decisions

The thinking behind the systems — not just what was built, but why.

Decision 01

Fixed Cinematic Cameras Over Free Camera

The game uses pre-scripted Cinemachine positions rather than a player-controlled camera. Fixed cameras let the design control exactly what the player sees — framing puzzles as intended, pacing information delivery, and preventing players from accidentally revealing solutions by looking around freely.

Decision 02

Action-Lock on Portal Transitions

The 3D↔2D dimension shift cannot be cancelled mid-animation. Making it interruptible would reduce it to a button press. The lock forces the player to experience the swap fully — the ink-spiral transition plays uninterrupted — reinforcing that they're crossing a real threshold, not toggling a filter.

Decision 03

State-Driven Camera Architecture

Rather than per-room camera logic, the CameraManager/CinemachineSwitcher/CameraTransformChanger system responds to gameplay state changes. Each state owns its camera configuration; the manager handles blending. This let level designers author new angles without touching code — critical when iterating fast in a 6-person team.
